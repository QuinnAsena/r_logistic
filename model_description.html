Model Description

Many population growth models exist in the biological and ecological literature. These equations are also used to describe a wide range of phenomena beyond population growth. Here we explore two simple versions of deterministic, discrete, density-dependent, logistic growth models.

By discrete we mean that we use difference equations (Equations 1 and 2 below) rather than a continuous growth equation (e.g. \(\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\!\left(1 - \frac{N}{K}\right)\)). Time is represented by discrete steps: \(N_t\) denotes the population at the current step and \(N_{t+1}\) the population at the next step. By deterministic we mean that no stochasticity (random variability) is included — for any given set of model parameters the trajectory is always the same. By density-dependent we mean that there is a defined limit (\(K\)) to a population’s growth: as the population approaches \(K\) the per-capita growth rate declines toward zero.

The aim of this page is to provide an interactive introduction to these population models, not a detailed mathematical treatment. The models can be set up to reproduce figures in, for example, @Gotelli2001, @Gillman2005, and @Gillman2009, and then experimented with. The app is suitable for lectures, tutorials, or independent study. All information here is compiled from the references listed at the end, which cover calculating \(r\), the relationship between \(r\) and \(\lambda\), and the effects of introducing stochasticity into the growth rate and carrying-capacity terms.

In the model tabs you can investigate the effects of different growth rates, starting populations, and carrying capacities on a logistically growing population. Note that these equations represent an idealised population in an idealised environment with a constant reproduction rate at every time-step.

In both model tabs, the upper plot shows population size over time and the lower plot shows the rate of population change, defined as \(\Delta N_t = N_{t+1} - N_t\). The rate at which a population grows depends on (a) the current size of the population (\(N\)) and (b) how close it is to carrying capacity (\(K\)). Growth is fastest near \(K/2\) (approximately, in the discrete model) because that is where the maximum number of reproducing individuals coincides with the most available resources.

Equation 1 [@Gotelli2001]

\[N_{t+1} = N_t + r_d\, N_t \left(1 - \frac{N_t}{K}\right)\]

Where:

  • \(N\) — population at time-step \(t\)
  • \(r_d\) — discrete growth factor
    • \(r = 0\): no growth
    • \(r > 0\): positive growth
    • \(r < 0\): negative growth (decline)
  • \(K\) — carrying capacity

This equation has three equilibria: \(N = K\) (at capacity), \(N = 0\) (extinction or extirpation), and \(r = 0\) (zero growth).

Equation 2 [@Gillman2005; @Gillman2009]

\[N_{t+1} = \lambda\, N_t \left(1 - \frac{N_t}{K}\right)\]

Where:

  • \(N\) — population at time-step \(t\)
  • \(\lambda\) — finite rate of increase
    • \(\lambda \gtrapprox 1.02\): positive growth (at \(N = 10\), \(K = 1000\))
    • \(\lambda \lessapprox 1.01\): negative growth (at \(N = 10\), \(K = 1000\))
    • \(\lambda = 1.03\): stable equilibrium (at \(N = 42\), \(K = 1442\))
  • \(K\) — carrying capacity

Unlike Equation 1, the equilibrium population size in Equation 2 depends on the growth rate \(\lambda\) (e.g. at \(\lambda = 2\) the population stabilises at \(K/2\)). Whether a given \(\lambda\) yields positive growth therefore depends on starting population and carrying capacity. This is not the case for the continuous logistic model, which is not covered here.

Limit Cycles and Chaos

Simple deterministic equations can give rise to surprisingly complex dynamics. While truly chaotic population trajectories are rare in nature, non-linear dynamics and chaos are widespread in other domains — and we can produce them here by adjusting growth rates.

Equation 1 (r discrete model tab) at \(N = 10\), \(K = 1000\):

Growth rate Behaviour
\(r_d < 2.000\) Damping oscillations toward \(K\)
\(2.000 < r_d < 2.449\) Two-point stable limit cycle
\(2.449 < r_d < 2.570\) Geometric increase in limit-cycle period (2, 4, 8, 16 …)
\(r_d > 2.570\) Aperiodic (chaotic) dynamics

Equation 2 (Lambda discrete model tab) at \(N = 10\), \(K = 1000\):

Growth rate Behaviour
\(\lambda < 3\) Damping oscillations toward \(K\)
\(3.0 < \lambda < 3.4\) Two-point stable limit cycle
\(3.4 < \lambda \lessapprox 3.6\) Geometric increase in limit-cycle period (2, 4, 8, 16 …)
\(\lambda \gtrapprox 3.6\) Aperiodic (chaotic) dynamics

Model Assumptions [@Gotelli2001]

The following assumptions underlie the general logistic growth model. The discrete models presented here relax the continuity assumption but share the remaining assumptions.

  • No time lags: the growth rate responds instantaneously to changes in population size and remains constant through time.
  • No age, size, or sex structure: birth and death rates do not vary with age, body size, or sex. Every individual is immediately reproductive with no distinct life stages. This assumption is most realistic for organisms such as bacteria or protozoa.
  • No genetic structure: all individuals share the same birth and death rates; no underlying genetic variation exists.
  • Constant carrying capacity: resource availability remains fixed through time.
  • Linear density dependence: for every individual added, the per-capita growth rate decreases linearly toward zero when \(N = K\).
  • No immigration or emigration: movement of individuals into or out of the population is assumed not to occur, or to be balanced.

More complex models relax these assumptions. In practice, violating some assumptions can substantially affect the accuracy of projections, but these simple models still illustrate the fundamental principles of logistic population growth.
